Supply Chain Analytics: Cooking Oil Sales Data

This project analyzes historical sales and inventory data for cooking oil to identify key performance indicators (KPIs), detect supply chain inefficiencies, and pinpoint critical periods requiring attention.

Key Performance Indicators (KPIs):

After initial data cleaning and preparation, the following core KPIs were calculated:

Total Demand:
The aggregate demand across the analyzed period was 49,660 units.

Average Inventory Turnover:
The average inventory turnover rate was 3.94, indicating how many times inventory was sold and replaced within the period.

Stockout Rate:
A significant 25.00% of the analyzed months experienced at least one stockout, highlighting a recurring issue.

Average Lead Time:
The average lead time for orders was 6.58 days.

Identification of Problem Areas:
Further analysis helped pinpoint specific instances of inefficiency:

High Stockout Occurrences:
Months with confirmed stockouts include 2024-06, 2024-07, 2024-11, 2024-12, 2025-07, and 2025-12.

Worst Inventory Efficiency (Lowest Turnover):
The months exhibiting the poorest inventory management (lowest turnover rates) were 2025-01 (2.71), 2025-02 (2.74), 2024-01 (2.77), 2024-02 (2.87), and 2025-03 (2.95).

Lead Time Problem Spikes:
Periods with exceptionally long lead times, indicating potential disruptions, were observed in 2025-12 (9 days), 2024-11 (9 days), 2024-06 (8 days), 2025-06 (8 days), and 2024-12 (8 days).

Insight:

Conditions During Stockouts:
A comparative analysis of months with and without stockouts revealed a clear pattern:

Higher Average Demand:
Months with stockouts typically experienced 2250 units of demand on average, significantly higher than the 2009 units in months without stockouts.

Lower Average Inventory On Hand:
Average inventory levels were 458 units during stockout months, notably lower than the 571 units in non-stockout months.

Longer Average Lead Time: 
Lead times averaged 8.0 days during stockouts, a considerable increase from the 6.1 days in non-stockout periods.

Higher Average Inventory Turnover:
Interestingly, stockout months also showed a higher average inventory turnover of 4.97, compared to 3.59 in non-stockout months. This suggests that while inventory levels were low, products were moving quickly, potentially contributing to the stockout if replenishment couldn't keep pace.

This quantitative insight strongly indicates that stockouts are often a combined result of surging demand, insufficient inventory buffers, and extended supply lead times.
